10 Ways to Speed Up Your Computer in 2026

✍️ Alex Chen 📅 March 8, 2026 ⏱ 8 min read

There's nothing more frustrating than a slow computer. Whether you're trying to finish a work project, stream your favorite show, or simply browse the web, a sluggish machine can kill your productivity and patience. The good news? Most performance issues can be resolved without spending a fortune on a new device.

In this comprehensive guide, we'll walk you through 10 proven methods to speed up your computer in 2026. These tips work for both Windows and macOS, and range from quick software tweaks to strategic hardware upgrades. Let's get started.

1. Disable Unnecessary Startup Programs

One of the biggest reasons your computer takes forever to boot is that too many programs launch automatically when you start your machine. Over time, as you install new software, many apps quietly add themselves to the startup list without asking.

On Windows: Press Ctrl + Shift + Esc to open Task Manager, click the "Startup" tab, and disable any programs you don't need running at boot. Look for apps like Spotify, Discord, Skype, and various update utilities — you can always launch them manually when needed.

On macOS: Go to System Settings → General → Login Items. Remove any apps that don't need to launch at startup. You'll be surprised how many unnecessary items accumulate over time.

💡 Pro Tip

If you're unsure whether a startup item is important, search for its name online before disabling it. Some entries are essential for your hardware drivers or security software to function properly.

2. Free Up Storage Space

When your hard drive or SSD is nearly full, your computer struggles to perform even basic tasks. The operating system needs free space for virtual memory, temporary files, and system updates. Aim to keep at least 15–20% of your drive free at all times.

Quick cleanup steps:

  • Empty your Recycle Bin or Trash — deleted files still take up space until permanently removed
  • Clear your browser cache and downloads folder
  • Uninstall programs you no longer use through Settings → Apps (Windows) or drag to Trash (macOS)
  • Use built-in tools like Windows Disk Cleanup or macOS Storage Management to find and remove junk files
  • Move large files (videos, photos, old projects) to an external drive or cloud storage

3. Upgrade to an SSD

If your computer still uses a traditional hard disk drive (HDD), upgrading to a solid-state drive (SSD) is hands-down the most impactful performance upgrade you can make. An SSD can make your computer boot in seconds instead of minutes, and applications will open almost instantly.

Modern NVMe SSDs are significantly faster than even SATA SSDs. A 1TB NVMe drive can now be purchased for under $60 in 2026, making this an incredibly cost-effective upgrade. Most desktop computers and many laptops allow SSD installation — check your device's specifications or a guide online for your specific model.

4. Add More RAM

RAM (Random Access Memory) is your computer's short-term memory. If you frequently run multiple applications simultaneously — say, a web browser with 20 tabs, a document editor, and a video call — you might be running low on RAM.

How much RAM do you need?

  • 8 GB: Minimum for comfortable everyday use (browsing, email, documents)
  • 16 GB: Ideal for multitasking, light photo/video editing, and gaming
  • 32 GB+: Best for heavy video editing, 3D rendering, or virtual machines

Check your current RAM usage through Task Manager (Windows) or Activity Monitor (macOS). If you're consistently using over 80% of your available RAM, an upgrade will make a noticeable difference.

5. Keep Your Operating System Updated

It might seem counterintuitive, but keeping your operating system updated is crucial for performance. Updates often include performance optimizations, bug fixes, and security patches that help your system run more efficiently. Both Windows and macOS release regular updates designed to improve stability and speed.

Set your system to automatically download and install updates, or at minimum, check for updates weekly. Don't ignore those update notifications — they exist for good reason.

6. Scan for Malware and Viruses

Malware, adware, and viruses can significantly slow down your computer by running hidden processes that consume CPU, RAM, and network resources. Even if you're careful about what you download, malware can sneak in through compromised websites, email attachments, or software bundles.

Run a full system scan using your built-in antivirus (Windows Defender is excellent in 2026) or a reputable third-party tool. Schedule regular scans — weekly is a good cadence — to catch threats early before they degrade performance.

7. Optimize Your Web Browser

For many people, the web browser is the most-used application on their computer. An unoptimized browser can consume enormous amounts of RAM and CPU power, especially with multiple tabs and extensions running.

Browser optimization tips:

  • Remove browser extensions you don't actively use — each extension runs processes in the background
  • Limit open tabs — use bookmarks or a tab manager extension instead of keeping dozens of tabs open
  • Clear your browsing data periodically (cache, cookies, history)
  • Enable hardware acceleration in your browser settings for smoother video playback and rendering
  • Consider using a lightweight browser profile for work tasks

8. Reduce Visual Effects

Modern operating systems come with various visual effects — animations, transparency, shadows, and smooth scrolling. While these look nice, they consume system resources, especially on older or budget hardware.

On Windows: Search for "Performance" in the Start menu, select "Adjust the appearance and performance of Windows," and choose "Adjust for best performance" or manually select which effects to keep.

On macOS: Go to System Settings → Accessibility → Display and enable "Reduce motion" and "Reduce transparency." These changes can make a notable difference on older Macs.

9. Adjust Power Settings

If you're using a laptop, your power settings might be throttling performance to save battery. When plugged in, you want your computer running at full power.

On Windows: Go to Settings → System → Power & Battery and select "Best performance" when plugged in. For desktops, ensure your power plan is set to "High performance" in the Control Panel.

On macOS: Recent versions handle power management automatically, but you can check Energy Saver preferences to ensure performance mode is activated when connected to power.

10. Consider a Fresh Install

If you've tried everything else and your computer is still slow, a clean reinstall of your operating system can work wonders. Over years of use, computers accumulate residual files, broken registry entries, stale drivers, and other digital debris that incrementally degrade performance.

A fresh install gives you a clean slate. Before proceeding, make sure to:

  • Back up all your important files to an external drive or cloud storage
  • Note down your installed applications so you can reinstall them
  • Save your browser bookmarks and passwords (most browsers sync these to the cloud)
  • Download the latest installation media from Microsoft or Apple's official website
⚠️ Important

A fresh install will erase everything on your system drive. Always create a complete backup before proceeding. Consider using our Data Backup Guide for step-by-step backup instructions.

Conclusion

A slow computer doesn't mean you need to buy a new one. By following these 10 tips — starting with simple software tweaks and graduating to hardware upgrades if needed — you can significantly boost your computer's performance and extend its useful life by years.

Start with the easiest changes first: disable startup programs, clean up storage, and optimize your browser. If those don't provide enough improvement, consider the hardware upgrades (SSD and RAM) which offer the best performance per dollar spent. And if all else fails, a clean operating system install can bring back that "new computer" feeling.
